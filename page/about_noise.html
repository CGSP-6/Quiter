Introduction

Noise, defined as unwanted or excessive sound, has been considered a pollutant since 1972, according to World Health Organization (WHO). Also, it is the second largest environmental health risk in Western Europe by the report published by WHO in 2011.

Image by Xiangyu Ren

When the limits provided in A-weighted decibels (dBA) by The United States National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders (NIDCD) are taken into consideration, at or below 70 dBA sounds are generally considered safe, while sounds at or above 85 dBA may cause hearing loss. Alongside the sound levels, exposure time is another cardinal factor for safety. The first recommended exposure limit (REL) for occupational noise exposure was offered by The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) in 1972. The latest REL revised in 1998 is an 8-hour time-weighted average using a 3-dB exchange rate . As shown in the figure below, noise level and exposure time are exponential.

Research Questions

Following are three research questions were addressed as the main frame of this study.
RQ-1: What are the benefits of green spaces for noise reduction?
RQ-2: What is the importance of commute-based noise measurements?
RQ-3: Can green spaces aid in noise reduction while commuting?

Hypotheses

Consequently, the aims are to provide the effectiveness of green spaces for noise reduction in public areas; the comparison point-based and commute-based noise measurements with their differences; noise reduction effects of green spaces on commutes. Lastly, three hypotheses were defined in this study.
H-1: Green spaces attenuate noise levels in public areas.
H-2: Commute-based measurements are more representative than point-based ones.
H-3: Commuting through green spaces reduces your exposure to noise.

Instrumentation and Equipment

We used the weather meters to record the wind speed and humidity. To record and manage our study area points, we used the application Map Plus. It allows custom online and offline maps to be added and viewed and supports all widely used offline maps as well as all popular online maps like Google Maps, Bing Maps, and OSM. It has an in-built GPS feature which we used to track the travelling routes we studied. It lets you export data to files of KML, GPX, etc., and share these files to iCloud, Email, or save them on your desktop computer. Additionally, to record the noise levels we used the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health’s (NIOSH) Sound Level Meter app which provides a readout of the sound level using the built-in microphone (or external microphone if used) and reports the instantaneous sound level in A, C, or Z-weighted decibels. It also reports the main metrics that are of importance for proper occupational noise measurements – mainly the run time (total time), the A-weighted Equivalent Sound Level (LAeq), which we used in our study.
